The 2026 FIFA World Cup had everything going for it. Three host nations, 16 cities, and a continent full of stadiums that had never seen international football at this scale. It was the largest tournament in history, and North America (with its college football cathedrals and its underserved soccer markets) offered something no previous host had: the chance to go somewhere genuinely new.

So FIFA went to Miami. Dallas. Los Angeles. The established markets, the proven venues, the choices no committee gets second-guessed for. It’s a reasonable list, and a familiar one. What it left out was the rest of the continent: dozens of the largest, loudest stadiums in North America, in cities that have never hosted anything like this.

What follows is a different version of the tournament, one that uses 35 venues instead of 16, keeps every team within a tight geographic pair for the entire group stage, and places games in college towns that will never see another event like this.

The data makes the case. The stadiums are why it matters.

The Distance Problem

In a tournament where teams play three group-stage games over two weeks, the distance between venues matters. Bosnia and Herzegovina covered 5,058 km. Toronto to Los Angeles to Seattle, more than a transatlantic flight. Czechia started in Guadalajara, flew to Atlanta, then returned to Mexico City: 4,544 km in a zigzag that ended almost where it began.

The Proposed Format

Every group gets two cities, geographically tight, so fans can basecamp in one place and day-trip to all three group games. Twelve groups, 24 cities in close pairs. The longest pair (Regina to Winnipeg) is 333 miles.

The knockout structure funnels toward three anchor venues: Azteca handles one semifinal half; BC Place in Vancouver handles the other; MetLife hosts the Final.

The World Cup’s greatest gift isn’t the games. It’s the geography. When Brazil plays in Lincoln, Nebraska, or Germany comes to Ann Arbor, those cities carry that moment for a generation.

The Cathedrals

These are some of the loudest, largest buildings in the world, and nearly all of them sat the tournament out.

Michigan Stadium holds 107,601, the largest in the Western Hemisphere; a group game in the Big House would draw the single biggest crowd of the entire World Cup. In Knoxville, Neyland Stadium seats its hundred thousand in orange-and-white checkerboard, with a fleet of fans who arrive by boat down the Tennessee River. At Auburn, a live eagle circles Jordan-Hare before kickoff and lands at midfield. Camp Randall empties its lungs between the third and fourth quarters, 76,000 people jumping in unison. At Clemson, the team touches a rock and sprints down a hill into what’s been called the most exciting 25 seconds in sports.

And then there’s the noise. On October 8, 1988, the celebration after a last-minute touchdown at LSU’s Tiger Stadium shook the ground hard enough to register on a seismograph across campus, college football’s literal Earthquake Game. In Lincoln, Memorial Stadium has sold out every single game since 1962, the longest such streak in the country, and on game days it becomes the third-largest city in Nebraska.

None of them hosted a match. FIFA chose the stadiums it already knew. The map above is what it could have chosen instead.

The Capacity Question

Capacity isn’t the trade-off you’d expect. The two formats seat almost exactly the same number of fans: 7.02 million against 7.00 million, a gap of 0.3%. The proposed format simply spreads that capacity across 35 venues instead of 16.

The spread is deliberate. The biggest stadiums anchor the group stage, where the most games reach the widest map, then capacity climbs toward the major-market venues for the knockouts.

The Honest Assessment

Every objection to this format was tested. Most collapsed.

College towns can’t handle the crowds. These programs run 100,000-person events every fall Saturday. The logistics are not the issue.

Revenue suffers without the big markets. Every proposed venue gets a top-seed game. A sold-out Jordan-Hare or Bryant-Denny at World Cup prices makes a serious revenue case.

The map leans east. It does. Three of the 35 sit on the West Coast, and California has one. The cathedrals cluster in the South and Midwest, and the format wants two close cities per group, so it leans with them. But the real ceiling was the count: even Beaver Stadium, the second-largest in the country, got cut when no big partner sat close enough. Phoenix and Vegas lost to bigger names with deeper history, and nothing already selected felt droppable. Save them for the 2038 bid.

What survives scrutiny: grass turnaround at college venues is a genuine operational challenge. It’s a negotiation, not a wall, but it’s real.

The venue-selection critique is ironclad. FIFA left spectacular stadiums unused because they were unfamiliar. The format critique survived better than expected.

FIFA’s choice was defensible. The 16 cities will host a fine tournament. But fine was available everywhere, and a continent only gets this once in a generation. Somewhere there’s a World Cup where Lincoln and Knoxville got their night under the lights, and Argentina came to the prairies. This one stays on paper. And it’s the version that would have spread the game faster, and across more of the map, than any World Cup before it.
